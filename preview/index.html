Infographics Assignment

A visual exploration of data — turning complex information into clear, compelling graphics.

Assignment

Infographics are visual representations of information. They can include numbers, text, images, or any combination of the three. For this infographic assignment, please explain how colonialism, imperialism, globalization, and/or militarism have impacted migration and/or (re)produced racial, sexual, gender, class, and global inequalities/hierarchies. Since the point of an infographic is to transfer knowledge and information quickly, the final poster should be informative, simple, engaging, and design-friendly. Be attentive to the order or flow of information.

Tools: Easelly, Piktochart, Infogram, Visme

Specifications

The infographic needs to visually communicate: (1) a thesis/main argument; (2) creative data representation (using charts, graphs, diagrams, flowcharts, maps, etc.) that supports the thesis, with proper citations of referenced materials; and (3) a conclusion.

Required content:

  • a title
  • eye-catching section headings
  • intentional color selection to advance your argument
  • a minimum of 1 original graph, chart, or table that you created yourself
  • a minimum of 4 graphics that amplify your argument
  • concise text to make and connect your arguments

Cite at least three credible sources, one of which must be from an assigned reading in Unit 3. Citations should appear in the footer of the infographic or some other discreet place, so that the sources can easily be checked.

Infographics should fit on a single page.

Infographic Structure

This assignment uses the same section structure as the example infographics (e.g. U.S. Militarism on Guam, The Ripple Effects of U.S. Imperialism on Central American Migration, and Militarism's Toll: The Vietnamese Diaspora and its Path to the US). Use these sections in order when you create your infographic. They map to the specs as follows: Introduction = thesis/main argument; Thematic sections = where your data representation (charts, graphs, maps, etc.) and citations go; Conclusion = conclusion.

1. Introduction (thesis/main argument)

Purpose: State your main argument or thesis in one or two paragraphs.

  • What is the central claim?
  • What forces or policies are you linking (e.g. U.S. militarism, imperialism, economic policy)?
  • What outcome are you explaining (e.g. migration, displacement, return migration)?

Example (Guam): "In cementing a foundation of defense in the West Pacific, the U.S. military implemented sociopolitical and economic policies on Guam… creating a perceived substantial dependence… In producing asymmetrical opportunities… the U.S. produced the migration of Guam's indigenous inhabitants from the island to the United States."

Example (Central America): "I argue that U.S. imperialism and militarism in Central America… have resulted in large-scale migration through the destabilization of local economies and the perpetuation of violence. Additionally, economic policies like NAFTA… have further exacerbated these issues."

2. Historical Context / Background (optional but recommended)

Purpose: Give a short timeline or set of key events so readers understand when and how the story unfolds.

  • Key dates (e.g. Treaty of Paris 1898, Civil War periods, policy start dates).
  • Brief labels so the reader can follow cause and effect.

Use a timeline, key dates list, or a short narrative—whatever fits your topic.

3. Thematic Sections (main body — data representation + citations)

Purpose: Break your argument into 2–4 thematic sections. Each section should have:

  • Narrative text that explains one part of your argument.
  • Data visualizations (charts, graphs, maps, tables) that support that part.

Naming: Use clear section titles that match your themes (e.g. Guam: Material & Discursive Militarism; Central America: Civil Wars, Return migration & deportations, NAFTA & economic displacement). Each section should make one main point and use at least one visualization.

4. Conclusion

Purpose: Tie the argument together in one or two paragraphs (the conclusion required by the specs). Restate how the forces you described lead to the outcomes; optionally note ongoing effects or implications.

5. References

Purpose: List all sources in a consistent citation style. Include reports, books, articles, census data. Place at the end of the infographic.

6. Description (optional)

Purpose: Short reflection: what each part of the infographic is meant to show, and what you learned.

Sample: U.S. Militarism on Guam

A sample infographic using the assignment structure

Introduction

In cementing a foundation of defense in the West Pacific, the U.S. military implemented sociopolitical and economic policies on Guam through material and discursive forms of militarism. The impacts of such forced an intertwining of Westernized and traditional ways of life for the CHamoru people, creating a perceived substantial dependence on U.S. structures on the island. In producing asymmetrical opportunities for advancement between American civilians and CHamorus, the U.S. produced the migration of Guam's indigenous inhabitants from the island to the United States.

Historical Context

  • 1898 — Treaty of Paris: Spanish territories, including Guam, ceded to U.S.
  • 1899 — Establishment of U.S. Naval Government; martial law in effect
  • 1941–44 — Japanese invasion and American reinvasion
  • 1950 — The Organic Act naturalizes all CHamorus as U.S. citizens; naval rule ends
  • 1940s — First wave of CHamoru military families settle in California

Material & Discursive Militarism — Education

The early U.S. Naval government appointed military officers and white American civilians as school administrators on the island, whose goal was for CHamoru citizens to "attain the standards of civilization." The marginalization of native teachers went hand-in-hand with increased military control and the Americanization of young students.
