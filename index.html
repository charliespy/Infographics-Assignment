Migration as Product of Empire

How militarism, imperialism, and war structure displacement and global inequalities.

Thesis

Wars force people from their homes; military networks control where people can go; and immigration laws choose who is let in based on politics rather than need. The Vietnam War's refugee routes (Espiritu), the linked struggles of Black Americans and Palestinians under empire (Rickford), and Cold War refugee rules (Wu) each show that the same powers that push people out also shape who gets in. "Rescue" stories hide the role these powers play in causing the crisis, letting global inequality continue without question.

1. Militarized Refugees

  • Vietnamese refugee flows were routed through the same U.S. military base network that had sustained the war.
  • Espiritu: By framing evacuations as "rescue and liberation," the U.S. masks the militarized nature of such operations (25).
  • War and empire produce displacement; imperial infrastructure then channels who moves where. Refugees are cast as proof of U.S. virtue even as their flight follows from U.S. intervention.

2. Imperialism & Racial Hierarchies

  • Imperialism connects sites of oppression.
  • Rickford: Black liberationists came to see Israel as an imperialist outpost and Palestinians as "ideological peers - subjects of state violence and occupation whose resistance offered a valuable model for African Americans" (53).
  • Same logic of racialized border-making: Indigenous removal and Black exclusion define the roots of U.S. immigration control.

3. Immigration Policy as Geopolitical Weapon

  • Wu: "U.S. geopolitical ambitions, especially the overarching goal of defeating communism everywhere, spurred the exodus and entry of refugees and asylees" (216).
  • People fleeing from communist states were often welcomed; people fleeing from U.S.-allied regimes were often denied. Cold War policy shaped who counted as a refugee.

Conclusion

Militarized refuge, imperialism and racial hierarchies, and immigration as geopolitical weapon show that colonialism, imperialism, militarism, and globalization actively structure migration. Wars create displacement; infrastructure channels movement; and ratives decide who belongs. Migration is a product of empire and a mechanism sustaining global inequalities. Migration should be a freedom, not be forced. This infographics tell us that we should cherish our freedom to migrate to places we wish.
